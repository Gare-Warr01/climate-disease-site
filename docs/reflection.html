Final Reflection

“Learning is not just about mastering concepts — it’s about discovering who you become through the work.”

This project challenged me in ways that felt both academic and personal.
It pushed me to bring together climate science, disease ecology, data analysis, and communication — and to build something meaningful from start to finish.
It showed me that when data, purpose, and clarity come together, science becomes something people can truly engage with.

What I Learned About Climate and Society

Before this course, most of my understanding of climate change came from the scientific side — greenhouse gases, warming trends, seasonal shifts, and datasets.
GEOG 3000 introduced a new layer: the human story.

I learned that:

  • Climate change doesn’t affect everyone equally
  • Vulnerability depends on wealth, location, infrastructure, and history
  • Environmental shifts show up in public health long before they appear in policy
  • Data is only useful if people can understand it

This project helped me see climate change not as a distant scientific issue, but as something that shapes daily life, health risks, and future choices.
It connected the science to the society that lives inside it.

Growth in Data Skills and Scientific Communication

This was the most technically challenging part of the course — and also where I grew the most.

I strengthened my ability to:

  • Clean messy, imperfect real-world datasets
  • Combine multiple sources into a unified workflow
  • Debug errors I didn’t expect
  • Build visualizations people can actually interpret
  • Host a public-facing website from scratch
  • Document each step so everything was reproducible

I also learned how to balance accuracy with accessibility — something that matters deeply in public health and climate communication.

The biggest shift was realizing that data is not the end of the process.
It’s the beginning of a conversation.

How This Project Supports My Professional Development

As a biotechnology student preparing for a career in research, lab work, and data-driven science, this project became a surprising bridge between disciplines.

It helped me grow in:

  • Analytical thinking — making sense of environmental and disease patterns
  • Technical skill — using R, ggplot2, and GitHub Pages
  • Communication — translating complex ideas into clear visuals
  • Professional independence — working through an entire project lifecycle

These skills are essential whether I end up in microbiology, epidemiology, data analysis, or broader biotech research.

This project wasn’t just classwork — it felt like a preview of the kind of scientific problem-solving I want to do in my career.

Leveraging This Project for My Future Career

Because this site is hosted publicly, it becomes a direct part of my professional identity.

I can use this project to:

  • Show employers my ability to work with real, complex datasets
  • Demonstrate that I can build reproducible workflows
  • Provide a polished example of my data visualization skills
  • Highlight my interest in climate health, epidemiology, and biotechnology
  • Strengthen interviews by discussing a real-world project with measurable skills
  • Link directly to it on my resume, LinkedIn, and applications

In biotech and research roles, employers want proof that you can take a problem, break it down, analyze it, and communicate it.

This website is that proof.

Key Takeaways From This Project

  • Climate change isn’t just environmental — it’s a public health issue.
  • Communicating science clearly is as important as running the analysis.
  • Real datasets are messy, but they teach you more than perfect examples ever could.
  • Reproducibility matters — both for credibility and future growth.
  • I’m capable of building full analytical projects from scratch.
  • This field — where climate, environment, and disease intersect — is something I want to explore more deeply in my career.

Looking Ahead

This project made climate change feel personal.
It made public health feel urgent.
And it made scientific communication feel meaningful.

As I move forward in my studies and career, I want to continue:

  • Asking questions that matter
  • Working with real-world data
  • Building projects that serve a purpose
  • Strengthening my communication skills
  • Staying curious about how biology and climate shape each other

This project reminded me why I chose science in the first place:
to understand the world more deeply, and to help people make sense of it too.

A Personal Note

This was one of the first projects where I felt genuinely proud of the work — not just because it was finished, but because it meant something.
It taught me patience, problem-solving, persistence, and the value of presenting science clearly.

More than anything, it helped me see the kind of scientist I’m becoming:
someone who values data, clarity, curiosity, and the connection between people and the planet.
