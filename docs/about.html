About This Project

This website is the applied, public-facing portion of my final project for
GEOG 3000: Climate Change in Science & Society at Utah Valley University.
The course examines climate change as both a physical science topic and a human, societal, and public-health issue.

Course Learning Focus

GEOG 3000 emphasizes:

  • Understanding how climate science works
  • Exploring vulnerability, exposure, and unequal impacts
  • Analyzing real datasets and communicating findings clearly
  • Connecting climate change to health, society, and everyday life

This project reflects those goals by taking a complex topic—climate and vector-borne disease—and presenting it in a way that is grounded in data and accessible to non-specialists.

Why I Chose This Topic

As a biotechnology student, I’ve always been drawn to topics that sit at the intersection of science, climate, and public health.
Vector-borne diseases—especially West Nile virus—depend heavily on environmental conditions such as temperature and moisture.

Studying how these patterns shift in a warming climate felt both scientifically meaningful and personally relevant.
It also offered a chance to practice real-world data analysis that aligns with my long-term career path in science.

A Capstone-Style Project

Although completed within a single course, this work was designed as a mini capstone:

  • Identifying a real, socially relevant question
  • Collecting and cleaning climate and disease datasets
  • Building a reproducible workflow in R
  • Creating visuals that explain complex ideas simply
  • Designing a public-facing product others can navigate and learn from

A separate written paper provides the full analysis, while this website focuses on communication—the part meant for students, instructors, and community members.

Why Utah?

Utah is where I live and study, and it has unique climate patterns:
long-term drought, sharp temperature swings, changing precipitation, and increasing heat extremes.

These conditions shape mosquito activity and influence West Nile virus risk.
Including a Utah-specific section helps connect national trends to a local, familiar landscape.

Tools and Skills Used

This project was built using:

  • R for data wrangling, visualization, and analysis
  • R Markdown for combining text, code, and graphics
  • tidyverse packages for data cleaning
  • ggplot2 for visualizations
  • GitHub Pages for hosting the site
  • Climate datasets from NOAA
  • Disease datasets from the CDC West Nile surveillance system

Completing the full workflow—from raw data to polished website—strengthened my data science, communication, and project-building skills.

How to Navigate This Site

Each tab walks through part of the project:

  • Climate Trends – long-term climate changes
  • Disease Trends – West Nile virus patterns
  • Climate–Disease Comparison – climate and disease side-by-side
  • Utah Focus – state-level climate and disease trends
  • How to Read These Graphs – help interpreting visuals
  • Methods – how the data was cleaned and analyzed
  • Reflection – what I learned along the way

You can explore the pages in order or jump directly into the sections that interest you.

Project Limitations & Transparency

This work reflects the scope of an undergraduate exploratory project.
It uses publicly available data, simple methods, and focuses on patterns rather than predictions.

My goal is clarity and transparency rather than forecasting or policy-making.
All code and steps are visible throughout the site to support reproducibility.
