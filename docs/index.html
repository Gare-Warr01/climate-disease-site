“Climate doesn’t just change averages. It shifts the conditions that diseases depend on.”

This site is my undergraduate capstone-style project on climate change and vector-borne disease in the United States, with a special focus on West Nile virus and a spotlight on Utah, where I live and study.

Using real climate and disease datasets, I explore a central guiding question:

How is a warming, shifting climate shaping the risk of mosquito-borne disease in the U.S., and what might that mean for places like Utah?

Overview

This website brings together climate records, public-health surveillance data, and a reproducible R workflow to explore three connected pieces:

  • Climate trends – how temperature, precipitation, and drought indicators have changed over time.
  • Disease trends – especially West Nile virus, one of the most widespread mosquito-borne diseases in the U.S.
  • Climate–disease comparisons – where patterns in climate and disease appear to move together in ways that might matter.

The goal is not to prove strict cause-and-effect. Instead, this project:

  • Looks for patterns and correlations that raise good questions.
  • Practices transparent, reproducible analysis in R.
  • Connects what we learned in GEOG 3000: Climate Change in Science and Society to real-world data.
  • Creates a professional, portfolio-ready project that communicates clearly to non-specialists.

Why This Matters

Vector-borne diseases like West Nile virus are tightly linked to environmental conditions:

  • Mosquitoes depend on temperature, moisture, and standing water.
  • Warmer temperatures can extend mosquito seasons and expand their geographic range.
  • Changing precipitation and drought patterns affect where mosquitoes breed and how often people and vectors come into contact.

As the climate warms and weather patterns shift, public health agencies, researchers, and communities need to understand how climate change might influence disease risk—and where vulnerabilities could grow.

This project is a small, student-level step in that direction: an example of how climate and disease data can be combined to start asking informed questions about risk in a changing climate.

Core Research Questions

This project is built around three simple but important questions:

  1. How is the U.S. climate changing over time?
  2. How are vector-borne diseases—especially West Nile virus—changing across space and time?
  3. Do climate and disease patterns appear to move together in ways that might matter for public health, especially in Utah?

Each tab on this site helps answer part of these questions.

Project Goals

More specifically, this site aims to:

  • Combine climate and disease datasets into a single analysis workflow in R.
  • Visualize long-term climate trends across the United States and selected regions.
  • Examine patterns in vector-borne disease (especially West Nile virus) over space and time.
  • Compare climate indicators and disease outcomes to see where interesting relationships appear.
  • Document the workflow so that, in principle, someone else could reproduce or extend the analysis.

These goals match the course emphasis on science, society, and communication—not just running numbers, but making them meaningful.

Data Overview

This project draws on several cleaned datasets stored as CSV files and read directly into R.

Climate Data

Climate-related datasets include:

  • Annual and/or seasonal temperature records
  • Precipitation data
  • Drought indicators, such as the Palmer Drought Severity Index (PDSI)
  • State-level or regional summaries, depending on availability

These datasets allow us to see how climate variables have changed over time and how those changes differ across space.

Disease Data

Disease-related datasets include:

  • Reported cases of West Nile virus from CDC and related surveillance sources
  • Data aggregated by year and state, so we can explore both temporal and spatial patterns

Together, the climate and disease datasets form the backbone of the visualizations and comparisons on this site.
Details on each dataset—including sources, key variables, and cleaning steps—are documented on the Data and Methods pages.

What This Project Can and Cannot Do

Because this is an exploratory undergraduate project, there are clear limits.

What It Cannot Do

  • It does not prove causal relationships between climate and disease.
  • It uses relatively simple methods (summaries, visualizations, and introductory modeling tools).
  • It does not attempt detailed forecasting, risk mapping, or policy modeling at a professional level.

What It Can Do

  • Provide a clear, organized view of climate and disease datasets in one place.
  • Highlight patterns and relationships that may be worth deeper study.
  • Demonstrate a transparent, reproducible R-based workflow that could be expanded with more advanced methods in future work.

This site is meant to be approachable: something that students, instructors, and interested visitors can explore without needing a background in advanced statistics—while still staying grounded in real data and careful methods.
