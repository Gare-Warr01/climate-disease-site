U.S. Climate & Vector-Borne Disease Project

Welcome

Welcome to my undergraduate project on climate change and vector-borne disease in the United States.
This site brings together climate data and disease data to ask a basic but important question:

How might a warming, changing climate affect the risk of diseases carried by mosquitoes and other vectors?

While the focus is national, I highlight Utah where possible, since that’s where I live and study.

What This Project Is About

This project uses real datasets and simple statistical tools in R to explore how:

  • Climate patterns (like temperature, precipitation, and drought) are changing over time.
  • Vector-borne diseases (like West Nile virus) vary across states and years.
  • These patterns might relate to each other, even in basic, exploratory ways.

The goal is not to “prove” a causal relationship, but to:

  • Look for patterns and correlations.
  • Practice transparent, reproducible analysis in R.
  • Build a foundation for thinking about how climate and health are connected.

Why This Matters

Vector-borne diseases depend heavily on environmental conditions:

  • Mosquitoes and other vectors are sensitive to temperature, moisture, and standing water.
  • Warmer temperatures can lengthen mosquito seasons or expand where they can survive.
  • Drought and precipitation can change where and how people and vectors interact.

As climate change continues, public health agencies, researchers, and communities need to understand how shifts in climate might change disease risk.
This project is a small, student-level step toward exploring that connection with real data.

Project Goals

More specifically, this site aims to:

  • Combine climate and disease datasets in one place.
  • Visualize long-term climate trends across the U.S.
  • Examine trends in vector-borne diseases over time and space.
  • Compare climate conditions and disease patterns to see where interesting relationships might appear.
  • Practice clear, documented R workflows so anyone could, in theory, rerun the analysis.

Data Overview

This project uses several types of data, including:

  • Climate data
    • Temperature (annual and seasonal patterns)
    • Precipitation and drought indicators
    • In some cases, state- or region-level summaries
  • Disease data
    • Reported cases of vector-borne diseases (especially West Nile virus)
    • Aggregated by year and location (e.g., state-level)

All data are cleaned and saved as CSV files so they can be easily loaded into R. Details on each dataset, including sources and cleaning steps, are described on the Data & Methods page.

What This Project Can and Cannot Do

This is an exploratory student project, so it has some limits:

  • It does not establish causal relationships between climate and disease.
  • It uses relatively simple methods (summaries, plots, basic models).
  • It focuses on clarity and transparency rather than advanced modeling.

What it does offer is:

  • A clear, organized look at climate and disease datasets.
  • A starting point for thinking about how climate change could influence vector-borne disease risk.
  • A reproducible R-based workflow that could be extended in future work.

Next Steps
