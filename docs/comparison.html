How Climate and Disease Move Together

This page brings the climate and vector-borne disease datasets together.
The goal is to test a simple idea:

As the U.S. has warmed and climate has shifted, have mosquito- and tick-borne diseases increased?

We use national annual data to build:

  • a combined climate–disease dataset,
  • visual comparisons over time, and
  • simple statistical tests relating climate indices to disease counts.

How to Read These Comparisons

  • Climate variables
    • temp_anomaly: annual temperature anomaly (°C) relative to a historical baseline.
    • pdsi: Palmer Drought Severity Index (negative = drier, positive = wetter).
  • Disease variables
    • total_cases: combined annual cases of West Nile, Lyme, Dengue, and Malaria.
    • Disease-specific counts by year and disease are stored in disease_by_climate.

Climate and Total Disease Over Time

What We See

  • The temperature anomaly line rises over time, especially from the 1990s onward, consistent with U.S. warming.
  • The total disease cases line is relatively low through the 1970s–1980s and then climbs sharply in recent decades.
  • Both lines trend upward, suggesting that as the climate has warmed, the combined burden of West Nile, Lyme, Dengue, and Malaria has also increased.

Interpretation

  • Moving from the 1970s to the 2010s and 2020s, average temperature anomalies become less negative and then positive, indicating a warmer climate.
  • Over those same decades, average total disease cases climb dramatically, especially after vector-borne diseases like West Nile virus and Lyme disease become more common in surveillance records.
  • This table does not prove causation, but it visually and numerically supports the idea that a warmer climate coincides with higher total vector-borne disease burden.

Disease-Specific Relationships with Temperature

Scatterplots: Cases vs Temperature Anomaly

What the Plots Suggest

  • Lyme disease tends to show a stronger upward trend with temperature: warmer years often have more Lyme cases.
  • West Nile virus and Malaria show weaker positive relationships overall.
  • Dengue cases are concentrated in more recent years and may show a different pattern, reminding us that diseases are shaped by:
    • travel and imported cases
    • local mosquito species and habitat
    • public health responses and control programs

Adding Drought: PDSI and Disease

Interpretation

  • For some diseases, especially Lyme, wetter years (higher PDSI) appear to line up with more cases, consistent with vector ecology and habitat.
  • For others, like West Nile, relationships can be more complex:
    • drought can concentrate birds and mosquitoes around limited water sources
    • but extreme dryness can also limit mosquito breeding

These patterns reinforce that climate–disease links are real but not simple.

Reading the “Proof Table”

  • Slope: how many extra cases are associated with a one-unit increase in the climate indicator (either °C anomaly or PDSI).
  • Correlation (r): strength and direction of the linear relationship (–1 to +1).
  • p-value: whether the slope is statistically distinguishable from zero (no relationship).

Rows highlighted in light green (and bold) have p < 0.05, meaning we can reject the null hypothesis of “no climate–disease association” for that indicator–disease pair.

In this dataset:

  • Lyme disease typically shows a stronger, positive relationship with both warming and moisture.
  • West Nile and Malaria show weaker but often positive associations with warming.
  • Dengue can show mixed or weaker relationships at this national scale, reflecting more complex drivers (travel, local transmission, and targeted interventions).

Questions and Next Steps

  1. Which diseases show the strongest statistical connection to climate in this dataset? Why might that be?
  2. How might land use, housing, and healthcare access strengthen or weaken these climate–disease relationships?
  3. If you zoomed into a single region (like Utah or the Northeast), would these patterns look stronger, weaker, or different?
  4. What kinds of public health actions (surveillance, mosquito control, education) could help break the link between a warming climate and increasing disease?
